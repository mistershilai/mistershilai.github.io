---
title: "The hype of medical AI"
description: "On current advancements in medical AI, their effectiveness, and their ethical boundaries."
date: 2026-06-13T12:00:00Z
tags: ["machine learning", "research ethics"]
---

Recently, a paper published in [Nature Medicine](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04431-5) found that large-language models (LLMs) built for nothing in particular outperform the ones built specifically for medicine. The researchers took two products marketed to clinicians, OpenEvidence and UpToDate Expert AI, and put them up against three frontier generalists: GPT-5.2, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and Claude Opus 4.6. The evaluation ran in three stages. First, five hundred MedQA questions of the kind that populate licensing exams. Then five hundred HealthBench items measuring how closely a model's answers track what clinicians consider good. Finally, a benchmark assembled from one hundred de-identified queries that physicians had actually typed into a general-purpose model during live clinical work, scored blind by twelve US clinicians. The generalists won all three. The specialized tools landed roughly where Google's AI Overview landed on the real queries, which is not the company a product wants to keep when it is being sold as purpose-built for medicine.

The reflex is to read this as a triumph of general intelligence, another data point in the long argument that scale and reasoning beat narrow training. That reading is not wrong, and it is not the interesting part. Consider what happened in a separate [randomized study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12920132/) of whether these models can help the general public. Tested on their own, the models correctly identified the underlying condition in almost ninety-five percent of cases. Yet when ordinary people used those same models to work through the same scenarios, they landed on the relevant condition in fewer than a third of cases, no better than a control group given no model at all. The knowledge was sitting in the system and it did not survive contact with a real user. What deserves attention, then, is the distance between what these benchmarks measure and what it takes to put a tool in front of a patient.

The authors were careful about this, more careful than the coverage that followed. A high MedQA score tells you a model can answer questions that look like exam questions. It tells you nothing about whether the tool integrates with an electronic health record, survives a regulatory audit, degrades safely when the input is messy, or has any coherent answer to the question of who is liable when it is confidently wrong. None of that shows up in a multiple-choice accuracy figure, and all of it is the actual work of deployment. The benchmark measures the easy and legible part of the problem, and the legible part is rarely where medicine breaks.

There is also a sharper objection, which is that the benchmarks may be measuring the wrong thing entirely. MedQA is multiple choice, and the questions have circulated long enough that contamination of training data is a live concern rather than a hypothetical one. HealthBench scores answers against a particular notion of what a good clinical answer looks like, which means a tool can lose for reasons of style and verbosity rather than for being less safe. It is at least possible that a terse, conservative tool that defers more often would score worse on alignment while being the better thing to hand a tired resident at three in the morning. Independent evaluation is exactly what this space has been missing, and this paper is a real contribution precisely because it supplies some. But a benchmark is an argument about what matters, and the argument here is far from settled.

The part that should bother anyone watching procurement is the inversion it implies. The specialized tools are the ones a hospital IT committee will approve, because clinical AI is a category that purchasing departments recognize and trust, complete with the reassuring vocabulary of validation and clinical grade. The generalist models, which tested better, arrive without that packaging and so face more friction getting through the door. The market is set up to prefer the label over the performance, and the label is doing a great deal of unearned work.

This is where the story stops being about which model wins and becomes a question of research ethics, which is the thread I find hardest to put down. The headline is that generalists beat specialists. The thing underneath the headline is that the specialists were entering clinical practice with almost no independent scrutiny in the first place. We built an industry around clinical decision support faster than we built the means to check whether it works, and the checking is only happening now, after these tools are already shaping triage and guideline interpretation in real settings. The hype did not wait for the evidence. In most fields that lag is an inconvenience. In medicine it is measured in patients.

So the lesson is not that you should reach for a general model the next time you want a clinical answer, tempting as the data makes that. The lesson is that specialized and clinical are marketing terms until somebody tests them, that the testing is slow and unglamorous and almost always arrives late, and that the enthusiasm always runs ahead of the proof. The useful response to a result like this is not to pick the new winner. It is to be more suspicious of the next thing that arrives wearing the right words, and to ask, before it touches anyone, whether anyone outside the company that built it has actually looked.
