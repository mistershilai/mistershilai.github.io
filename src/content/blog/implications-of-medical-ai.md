---
title: "The hype of medical AI"
description: "On current advancements in medical AI, their effectiveness, and their ethical boundaries."
date: 2026-06-13T12:00:00Z
tags: ["machine learning", "research ethics"]
---

Recently, a paper published in [Nature Medicine](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04431-5) found that large-language models (LLMs) built for nothing in particular performed at least as well as the ones built specifically for medicine. The researchers took two products marketed to clinicians, OpenEvidence and UpToDate Expert AI, and put them up against three frontier generalists: GPT-5.2, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and Claude Opus 4.6. The evaluation ran in three stages. First, five hundred MedQA questions of the kind that populate licensing exams. Then five hundred HealthBench items measuring how closely a model's answers track what clinicians consider good. Finally, a benchmark assembled from one hundred de-identified queries that physicians had actually typed into a general-purpose model during live clinical work, scored blind by twelve US clinicians. The generalists edged out the specialists on all three, and the specialized tools landed closer to Google's AI Overview than many in the field would have expected.

The reflex is to read this as a triumph of general intelligence, another data point in the long argument that scale and reasoning beat narrow training. That reading is not wrong, and it is not the interesting part. Consider what happened in a separate [randomized study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12920132/) of whether these models can help the general public. Tested on their own, the models correctly identified the underlying condition in almost ninety-five percent of cases. Yet when ordinary people used those same models to work through the same scenarios, they landed on the relevant condition in fewer than a third of cases, no better than a control group given no model at all. The knowledge was sitting in the system and it did not always survive contact with a real user. What deserves attention, then, is the distance between what these benchmarks measure and what it takes to put a tool in front of a patient.

The authors were careful about this, and worth reading on it. A high MedQA score tells you a model can answer questions that look like exam questions. It is quieter on whether the tool integrates with an electronic health record, holds up under a regulatory audit, degrades gracefully when the input is messy, or has a clear answer to the question of who is accountable when it is confidently wrong. None of that shows up in a multiple-choice accuracy figure, and most of it is the actual work of deployment. The benchmark measures the legible part of the problem, and the legible part is often not where medicine breaks.

There is also a fairer objection, which is that the benchmarks may not be measuring the most decision-relevant thing. MedQA is multiple choice, and the questions have circulated long enough that training-data contamination is a live concern. HealthBench scores answers against a particular notion of what a good clinical answer looks like, which means a tool can lose for reasons of style and verbosity rather than for being less safe. It is at least possible that a terse, conservative tool that defers more often would score worse on alignment while being the better thing to hand a tired resident at three in the morning. Independent evaluation is exactly what this space has needed more of, and this paper is a real contribution precisely because it supplies some. But a benchmark is an argument about what matters, and the argument here is still being worked out.

The part worth flagging for procurement is the inversion it points to. Specialized tools tend to have an easier path through hospital IT committees because clinical AI is a familiar category with vocabulary buyers know how to weigh, complete with the reassuring language of validation and clinical grade. Generalist models arrive without that packaging and so face more friction getting through the door. Findings like this suggest the label and the measured performance can move somewhat independently, which is a useful thing to know on both sides of a purchase order.

This is where the story stops being about which model wins and becomes a question of research practice, which is the thread I find hardest to put down. The headline is that generalists held their own against specialists. The thing underneath is that the field has been deploying specialized tools faster than it has been independently evaluating them. We've built an industry around clinical decision support a step ahead of the means to check it, and the checking is happening now, often in parallel with tools that are already shaping triage and guideline interpretation in real settings. Evidence has been catching up to deployment rather than preceding it. In most fields that order of operations is awkward. In medicine the asymmetry matters more, which is exactly why work like this paper is worth doing.

So the lesson is not that you should reach for a general model the next time you want a clinical answer, tempting as the data makes that. The lesson is that specialized and clinical are claims that need independent testing before they become evidence, that the testing is slow and unglamorous and often arrives after the tools do, and that enthusiasm tends to run ahead of proof. The useful response to a result like this is not to pick the new winner. It is to treat independent evaluation as part of what clinical readiness should mean, and to ask, before any tool reaches a patient, whether someone outside the team that built it has had a real chance to look.
