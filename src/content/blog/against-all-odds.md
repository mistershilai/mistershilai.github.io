The first time I encountered [Pardis Sabeti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pardis_Sabeti), I did not know her name and did not particularly care to. I was a freshman in high school, and some required class had assigned a series of statistics videos from Annenberg Learner, the kind of public-television courseware that arrives in your life with all the glamour of a fire-safety filmstrip. The series was called [*Against All Odds: Inside Statistics*](https://www.learner.org/series/against-all-odds-inside-statistics/). I remember stemplots and standard deviations and a host who seemed a little too sincerely delighted by histograms of lightning strikes. What I did not register, because I had no reason to, was that the woman walking me through two-way tables was an active outbreak scientist, and that one of those tutorials was quietly built around [her own research into genetic resistance to Lassa fever in West Africa](https://www.learner.org/series/against-all-odds-inside-statistics/inference-for-two-way-tables/). The statistics were a Trojan horse. She had filmed those lectures in 2013, on the eve of the deadliest Ebola outbreak in recorded history, and by the time the assignment reached me years later she had already lived through it. I sat through a full unit of her explaining inference without the faintest idea that the host had spent the intervening years at the center of an epidemic.

I learned all of that much later, and in pieces. Sabeti is a computational geneticist at the Broad Institute and Harvard who built her early reputation on statistical tests for detecting natural selection in the human genome, then turned those methods on the pathogens that select us in return. She also fronts a rock band, which is the sort of detail that makes more sense once you have watched her teach probability with the energy of someone who genuinely cannot believe how interesting it all is. What I find most instructive about her, more than any single line in her bibliography, is that she trained as a physician and then declined to choose between the clinic and the computer, treating medicine and computation as one practice rather than two careers to pick between.

The outbreak itself I came to through a film. [FRONTLINE's *Outbreak*](https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/frontline/documentary/outbreak/), directed by Dan Edge and aired in 2015, remains one of my favorite documentaries, which is a strange thing to say about ninety minutes of nearly unbroken grief. It traces the 2014 epidemic from a one-year-old boy in a Guinean village to the wards of Sierra Leone and the slums of Monrovia, and its central question is not how the virus killed but why the response failed for so long. Much of it takes place at Kenema Government Hospital, where the government chose to treat Ebola patients because Kenema already ran a ward for Lassa fever, a related hemorrhagic disease. Within days the hospital was overwhelmed, and then the nurses began to die. The deputy head nurse, Josephine Sellu, describes counting bodies in the morgue and wondering whether the world was ending. Thirty-seven health workers died at that one hospital: doctors, nurses, porters, cleaners, security guards, lab technicians. Rumors spread through the town that Ebola was a hoax invented by doctors to steal blood, and crowds threw stones at the hospital and threatened to burn it down. The film ends with the names of the dead read aloud, nurses and lab techs and small children, and it is the most devastating thing I have ever watched precisely because it refuses to convert the loss into a lesson.

Here is where the statistics videos and the documentary close into a single circuit. The Lassa fever program at Kenema, the one featured in that tutorial I half-watched at fourteen, was Sabeti's foothold in Sierra Leone for years before Ebola arrived. When the outbreak came, that program and its people became the front line. In August 2014 her lab [published ninety-nine Ebola genomes](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1259657) drawn from seventy-eight patients at Kenema in *Science*, work that let researchers track in something close to real time how the virus was moving and mutating across West Africa. They released the sequences immediately, before the paper was even written, on the conviction that hoarding data during an epidemic is its own form of harm. By the time the paper appeared, five of its co-authors had died of the disease they had helped to sequence: Mohamed Fullah, Mbalu Fonnie, Alex Moigboi, Alice Kovoma, and [Sheik Humarr Khan](<https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(14)61428-8/fulltext>), the physician who led the Kenema program and whom Sabeti named as a co-senior author and a friend. The data and the dying were never two separate stories. The genomes came from the same ward, gathered by the same hands, that the outbreak was steadily emptying.

I think about that circuit often, because the lesson of it is easy to state and hard to live by. An outbreak in a remote district is never a local event for long. The same connective tissue that moves people and goods moves pathogens, and a virus that goes unseen in one place for a few weeks can be on three continents by the time anyone gives it a name. What stands between a contained outbreak and a global one is rarely heroics. It is the quiet scaffolding that has to be built before the fact: surveillance that catches an anomaly early, laboratories that can confirm it, clinicians and data systems already in place when the first patient walks in. That scaffolding is unglamorous, it is expensive, and it is the first thing to be treated as optional in the long stretches between emergencies. Experts put the odds of another pandemic in the next quarter century at roughly even. The closing argument of *Outbreak*, delivered by the WHO's Bruce Aylward, was that Ebola was not an exception but a precedent, and that more of these are coming whether we are ready or not.

The lesson I take from 2014 is not the one the genre usually wants to sell. It was never that the world will ride in to rescue a forgotten place. The world was late, the international machinery hesitated for months, and the people who actually held the line were local clinicians improvising with whatever they had. I am instinctively skeptical of the idea that good intentions routed through large institutions reliably reach the ward, and the history here does little to soften that skepticism. What carried real weight in this story was unglamorous and built in advance: a Lassa ward that already existed, a surveillance system funded the year before anyone was watching, a sequencing pipeline ready to run, a team that knew the terrain. Sabeti's instinct to release the genomes the moment they existed was of a piece with that ethic. So was Khan's clinic. The dramatic part of an outbreak is almost never the part that decides how it ends.

I think about those statistics videos more than I expected to. The woman I barely registered at fourteen had filmed them just before the outbreak that would take the lives of several of her colleagues and reshape her own, and she was walking me cheerfully through two-way tables using the very Lassa research that would soon put her at the center of it. By the time the assignment reached me, all of it had already happened, and I knew none of it. That is the part I keep returning to. The people whose work decides how an outbreak ends rarely look, at the time, like they are doing anything momentous. They are building the boring, durable things in the years when no one is watching. I am grateful, in a roundabout way, to whatever teacher made me watch.
